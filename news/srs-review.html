1. Why Review Requirements Now — Five Facts About Software Errors

Before presenting the team's findings, it is worth establishing why a formal requirements-phase review is not optional but critical. The following five empirical facts, drawn from decades of software engineering research, make the case.

Fact 1
The later a defect is found, the more expensive it is to fix

The cost of correcting a software error grows dramatically as the project advances through its lifecycle. A defect that costs 0.1–0.2 units to fix during requirements can cost 20 units if it survives to the maintenance phase — a factor of 100× or more.

Requirements0.1 – 0.2

Fact 2
Many errors remain latent long after they are introduced

Boehm's analysis of TRW projects found that 54% of all detected errors were not discovered until after the coding and unit testing phases. Worse, the vast majority of these late-discovered errors (45% of all errors) actually originated in the requirements and design phases, while coding-phase errors accounted for only 9%.

This means the most dangerous errors — those introduced earliest and caught latest — are precisely the ones a requirements review is designed to intercept.

Fact 3
The requirements phase produces a disproportionate share of errors

DeMarco's research report indicated that 56% of all detected errors can be traced back to the requirements phase. A separate investigation by AIRMICS found over 500 errors in a single U.S. military management information system's requirements specification document — and that was from just one project in one audit.

Fact 4
Requirements errors are characterised by omission, inconsistency, and ambiguity

The U.S. Naval Research Laboratory's field study on the A-7E flight operations software found that 77% of requirements errors were attributable to vagueness — specifically omission, inconsistency, and ambiguity. The breakdown:

Requirements errors can be caught — inspection is the most effective method

Basili and Weiss's data confirmed that 33% of requirements errors in the A-7E software definition documents were found through manual inspection. Celko further argued that automated analysis tools could detect a significant additional proportion of errors from the SRS.

The Case for Requirements-Phase Inspection

Taken together, these five facts establish an unambiguous conclusion: the requirements phase is where most errors originate, where they are cheapest to fix, and where inspection is the most effective detection method. Deferring review to later phases does not save effort — it multiplies it.

For our SRS, this means each team member should independently review the document before it advances to the next version. The review should target the four dominant error types identified by the A-7E study: incorrect facts, omissions, inconsistencies, and ambiguities.

Effective requirements-phase review practices include:

  • Formal inspection (Fagan inspection): A structured walkthrough where each reviewer independently reads the SRS, records defects, then the team meets to consolidate findings. The most reliable method for catching omissions and inconsistencies.
  • Cross-referencing against system constraints: Checking every use case against known interface contracts (IF1, IF2), deployment contexts (clinical vs. home), and actor definitions to ensure nothing is contradicted or left undefined.
  • Traceability checking: Verifying that every requirement can be traced to a stakeholder need, and that every stakeholder need has at least one requirement covering it. Missing traceability signals omissions.
  • Consistency matrix: Building a matrix of all actors, terms, and interfaces mentioned across use cases to check that the same concept is never referred to by two different names (or two concepts by the same name).
  • Automated static analysis: Where tooling is available, running the SRS through consistency checkers or natural language analysis to flag ambiguous phrasing, undefined terms, or passive-voice requirements that obscure responsibility.

2. Member Reviews

Each team member independently inspects the SRS Draft (v0.1) and documents their findings below. Reviews are structured by reviewer, with each issue categorised by type (omission, inconsistency, ambiguity, or incorrect fact).

3. Consolidated Summary

The consolidated summary will be compiled once all five team members have submitted their individual reviews. It will categorise all findings by error type (omission, inconsistency, ambiguity, incorrect fact), prioritise them by severity, and propose a revision roadmap for SRS v0.2.
