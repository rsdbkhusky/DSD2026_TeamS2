Roles Assigned Across S2

Every seat in Team S2 has a named owner. This post explains what each role means in practice — particularly how the Shadow PM and cross-testing model work across a distributed team.

Following the initial team formation, the five S2 roles have been formally assigned. The DSD role structure is not purely nominal — each position carries specific responsibilities that are designed for a team operating across two time zones, two institutions, and two languages.

Project Manager — Zhiqi Zhang

The PM owns the sprint cadence: planning, tracking, and reporting. Zhiqi represents S2 at All-Site Meetings (ASMs) — the three mandatory synchronous cross-project meetings held via VooV (WeMeet). When a decision affects another team, the PM is the first contact.

Vice PM (Shadow PM) — João Neves

The Vice PM role in DSD is more than a deputy — it is a parallel leadership position. João holds full authority on the Portuguese side of the team during the hours when the PM (in China) is offline. At the end of each PT working day, João posts a concise standup update to the GitHub Wiki in the format: done / in progress / blockers. This eliminates the need for a synchronous daily meeting across the timezone gap.

System Architect — Haobo Lin

The System Architect's most important Sprint 1 task is co-authoring IF1 with V1's System Architect. IF1 defines the exact contract between the Sensor and Server layers — the JSON data schema, sampling rate, error codes, and MQTT topic structure. Once frozen at the end of Sprint 1, IF1 becomes the immutable specification that all downstream teams build against. No ad-hoc changes after the freeze date — any modification requires a formal joint agreement.

Programmers — Pengxu Chen & Tianze Wang

S2 has two Programmers and no dedicated Tester. Quality control is enforced through a formal cross-testing model: Pengxu formally reviews and tests Tianze's code, and Tianze formally reviews and tests Pengxu's code. Every critical module ships with independent verification from the other programmer before it is considered done.

Both programmers work on the same codebase, but the cross-testing constraint means that no single programmer's output goes unreviewed. In a small team without a QA role, this is the mechanism that catches integration errors before they reach other teams.
